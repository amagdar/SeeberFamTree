Historical Narrative

The Seeber family story spans four branches—Teitelbaum, Schlesinger, Endervelt, and Swerbilov—whose journeys move from Hungary, Poland, and Russia to the United States in the early 20th century. The Teitelbaum family left Cushow, Hungary around 1921–1922, seeking safety from conscription and opportunity in America. They attended night school, worked in local factories, and found community through the Hungarian social club MUNKOS OTHUN. The Schlesinger siblings, orphaned in Budapest after losing both parents to tuberculosis, were sponsored to the U.S. by their eldest brother Carl; their resilience and trade skills grew into successful enterprise supporting the WWII effort. The Endervelts emigrated from Poland in 1904, overcoming poverty through early work, education, and artistic talent—while maintaining close ties despite geographic distance. From Orsha, Russia, the Swerbilov line arrived to avoid conscription, building livelihoods in Brooklyn and Annapolis and staying connected through the Orsha Young Friends Benevolent Society. Across these branches, common threads are migration for safety and opportunity, reliance on community sponsorship, commitment to education and enterprise, and enduring intergenerational family bonds.
